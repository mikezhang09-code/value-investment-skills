# Moat Analysis Framework

A competitive moat is the sustainable advantage that allows a company to earn above-average returns on capital for many years. Without a moat, competition will erode returns toward the cost of capital over time. Identifying and assessing moat durability is the most important qualitative judgment in value investing.

---

## The Five Sources of Moat

### 1. Intangible Assets
Brands, patents, regulatory licenses, or trade secrets that let a company charge premium prices or block competition.

**Brand moat:**
- Customers pay a premium specifically because of the brand name
- Brand commands loyalty even when alternatives exist at lower prices
- Examples: Coca-Cola, Louis Vuitton (LVMH), Moutai (Kweichow)
- Test: Would customers switch if a generic offered the same product at 20% lower price? If no → strong brand moat

**Patent/IP moat:**
- Legal protection for products (typically 20 years for patents)
- Strong in pharma, specialty chemicals, medical devices
- Weakness: patents expire; need pipeline to maintain
- Test: When key patents expire, how much revenue is at risk?

**Regulatory license moat:**
- Government-granted monopoly or near-monopoly
- Broadcasters, utilities, financial licenses, airport slots
- Examples: Banks (banking licenses), NYSE, toll roads
- Strength: very durable, hard to replicate
- Weakness: subject to regulatory change; political risk

**Assessment questions:**
- Can a competitor replicate this advantage with enough money and time?
- Is the brand/IP becoming stronger or weaker over time?
- Are there substitute products that undermine the advantage?

---

### 2. Cost Advantage
The ability to produce goods/services at structurally lower cost than competitors, enabling either lower prices or higher margins.

**Scale-based cost advantage:**
- Fixed costs spread over more units → lower unit cost
- Applies to: manufacturing, distribution networks, software
- Examples: Amazon logistics network, TSMC semiconductor fabs, Walmart distribution
- Test: Does cost per unit fall as volume grows? How much cheaper than the #2 player?

**Location-based cost advantage:**
- Unique access to cheap inputs (labor, materials, energy) or proximity to customers
- Mining companies near high-grade deposits
- Companies near cheap power sources (aluminum smelting)
- Strength: highly durable; location doesn't move
- Weakness: limited scalability

**Process-based cost advantage:**
- Proprietary manufacturing processes that are difficult to replicate
- Operational excellence embedded in culture and systems
- Examples: Toyota Production System
- Weakness: can be copied given enough time and investment

**Assessment questions:**
- What is the cost differential vs. next-best competitor?
- How sustainable is this cost advantage if competitors invest heavily?
- Is the cost advantage shrinking due to technology change?

---

### 3. Switching Costs
The friction (financial, operational, psychological) that makes customers reluctant to switch to a competitor, even when alternatives exist.

**High switching cost indicators:**
- Product deeply embedded in customer workflows (ERP software, core banking systems)
- Data/history locked into the platform
- Employees trained on the system; retraining is expensive
- Long-term contracts with penalties
- Significant productivity loss during transition

**Examples:**
- SAP, Oracle (enterprise software): customers have used these for decades; switching costs enormous
- Bloomberg Terminal: deep workflow integration in financial services
- Medical devices: surgeons trained on specific platforms
- Industrial automation: reconfiguring factory lines is enormously expensive

**Assessment questions:**
- What is the realistic cost (in money and time) for a customer to switch?
- How long do customers typically stay?
- Are switching costs increasing or decreasing (technology can reduce them)?
- What % of revenue is recurring?

---

### 4. Network Effects
The product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Each new user adds value for all existing users.

**Direct network effects:**
- Value increases directly with number of users
- Examples: WhatsApp, Visa/Mastercard payment network, stock exchanges
- Test: Would you use this product if only 100 people used it? 1 million? 1 billion?

**Indirect network effects (two-sided platforms):**
- Two distinct user groups — each side benefits from more participants on the other
- Examples: Uber (riders + drivers), Airbnb (hosts + guests), app stores (users + developers)
- Winner-take-all dynamics are common
- Weakness: two-sided networks can tip to a competitor if they successfully onboard one side

**Data network effects:**
- More users generate more data → better AI/algorithms → better product → more users
- Examples: Google Search, TikTok recommendation engine, Meituan delivery optimization
- Very powerful in AI-era companies

**Assessment questions:**
- What type of network effect is this? How strong?
- Is there geographic segmentation (allows local competitors to thrive)?
- Has the network effect been tested — did the company maintain share during competitive attacks?

---

### 5. Efficient Scale
The market is only large enough to support one or a few providers. Entering would reduce returns for all players — making entry economically irrational.

**Examples:**
- Local utilities (gas, water, electricity)
- Regional airports
- Specialized industrial niches where one operator serves the entire addressable market
- Pipeline infrastructure serving a specific geography

**Assessment questions:**
- How large is the addressable market?
- Would a second entrant destroy industry economics?
- Are there geographic or regulatory barriers reinforcing the efficient scale?

## Moat Durability Assessment

A moat can be wide, narrow, or nonexistent — and its width can change over time.

| Durability | Definition | Typical Moat Life | Examples |
|------------|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| **Wide** | Structural advantages unlikely to erode for 20+ years | 20–50+ years | Coca-Cola brand, Visa network, TSMC process leadership |
| **Narrow** | Real advantages but more vulnerable to disruption or competition | 5–15 years | Most patent-based moats, regional brand advantages |
| **None** | Competitive advantages are easily replicated or disappearing | < 5 years | Commodity producers, companies in structural decline |
| **Negative** | Business is in permanent structural decline | Shrinking | Print media, legacy tech, declining brands |

**Signs a moat is widening:**
- Market share growing in core segments
- Pricing power demonstrated (raising prices without volume loss)
- Customer retention rates stable or improving
- Returns on capital stable or improving despite competitive pressure

**Signs a moat is eroding:**
- Market share losses to new entrants
- Gross margin compression despite volume growth
- Rising customer acquisition costs
- Disruptive technology making the business model obsolete

## Industry-Specific Moat Patterns

### Consumer Brands
- Primary moat: brand + switching costs (habit)
- Look for: decades of market share stability, pricing power vs. private label
- Risk: millennial/Gen Z brand loyalty is weaker; social media can destroy brands fast

### Financial Services (Banks, Insurance)
- Moats: customer switching costs + regulatory license + scale
- Best banks: low-cost deposit franchise + underwriting discipline
- Insurance: combined ratio < 95% + float investment returns

### Technology (Enterprise Software)
- Moats: switching costs + network effects + data advantages
- Best businesses: high ARR, low churn, expanding revenue from existing customers
- Watch for: open-source disruption, cloud shifts changing switching cost dynamics

### Healthcare (Pharma, Medical Devices)
- Moats: IP/patents + regulatory barriers + switching costs (doctor habits)
- Pharma: patent cliff risk is real — pipeline health is critical
- Medical devices: strong moats once surgeons/hospitals adopt

### Industrials / Specialty Manufacturing
- Moats: scale + process advantages + customer switching costs
- Best: mission-critical parts (expensive to switch, small % of customer cost)
- Watch for: commodity inputs, tariff/trade exposure

### Technology Hardware (Semiconductors)
- Moats: IP + scale + technology leadership
- TSMC-type: process technology moat + customer lock-in
- Fabless: IP and architecture moat (Qualcomm, ARM)

### China A-Shares / HK Consumer
- Strong moats: Kweichow Moutai (baijiu brand), China Mobile (scale + license)
- Watch for: SOE capital allocation inefficiency
- Premium brands in China often have wide moats due to aspirational consumer culture

## Moat Scorecard

Rate each dimension 1–5, then calculate overall moat strength:

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Evidence |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Pricing power (can raise prices?) | | |
| Customer retention / low churn | | |
| Market share stability over 5–10 years | | |
| Returns on capital vs. industry peers | | |
| Barriers to competitive entry | | |
| Moat trend (widening/stable/eroding) | | |
| **Total Moat Score** | **/30** | |

**Interpretation:**
- 24–30: Wide moat — exceptional business quality
- 18–23: Narrow moat — good business, requires ongoing monitoring
- 10–17: Questionable moat — price must compensate for vulnerability
- < 10: No moat — avoid unless extremely cheap (Graham deep value)

## The Munger Inversion: Moat Stress Test

For every moat identified, apply inversion:

**"What would destroy this moat in 10 years?"**

For each moat type, common threats:
- **Brand moats**: Social media scandals, quality failures, generational taste shifts, private label competition
- **Cost moats**: New entrant with better technology making current scale irrelevant, commodity cost spikes
- **Switching cost moats**: Open-source alternatives, API-driven architecture reducing lock-in, regulatory unbundling
- **Network effects**: Geographic fracturing, better-funded rival acquiring one side of the network, government-mandated interoperability
- **Regulatory moats**: Policy change, deregulation, new administration

If threats are **concrete and near-term** → narrow moat at best
If threats are **speculative and distant** → likely wide moat
