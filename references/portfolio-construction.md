# Portfolio Construction for Value Investors

A concentrated portfolio of 10–20 high-conviction positions, built with margin of safety, managed with patience. The goal is to beat the market over rolling 5-year periods while preserving capital in downturns.

---

## Portfolio Philosophy

**Concentration over diversification:**
Buffett: "Diversification is protection against ignorance. It makes little sense if you know what you are doing."
Graham: Even the defensive investor needs only 10–30 stocks; more is not better.

**High conviction, not speculation:**
Each position must be justified by a full analysis (Steps 1–7 in the skill). If you can't articulate why you own it in two sentences, you shouldn't own it.

**Time horizon:**
Target holding period: 5–10 years minimum. Short-term price movements are noise. The investment case is about business earnings power over years, not quarters.

---

## Portfolio Structure

### Position Count
| Portfolio Size | Recommended Positions | Rationale |
|---------------|-----------------------|-----------|
| Core (high-conviction) | 10–15 names | Meaningful diversification; each position matters |
| Extended (more ideas) | 16–20 names | Captures more opportunities without over-diluting |
| Maximum | 20 names | Beyond this, ideas become less differentiated |

### Position Sizing Framework

Position size reflects conviction level and margin of safety:

| Conviction Level | Criteria | Target Weight |
|-----------------|----------|--------------|
| **High conviction** | Wide moat + > 35% margin of safety + deep understanding | 8–15% |
| **Moderate conviction** | Narrow moat + 25–35% MOS + good understanding | 5–8% |
| **Starter position** | Establishing a position; still building conviction | 2–5% |
| **Monitoring position** | Great business, minimal MOS; await price decline | 1–2% |

**Rules:**
- No single position > 15% at cost (can grow larger via appreciation — fine to hold)
- No single position > 20% of portfolio at market value (trim if crosses this)
- Minimum meaningful position: 3% (below this, it won't move the portfolio needle)

### Geographic Diversification

Recommended allocation ranges for a global value portfolio:

| Market | Target Range | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| US (NYSE/NASDAQ) | 40–60% | Deepest market, best disclosure, highest valuations |
| Hong Kong | 10–25% | Access to China growth + HK conglomerates |
| China A-shares | 5–15% | Higher risk/reward; strong domestic brands |
| India | 5–15% | Fast-growing market; premium valuations in quality names |
| Singapore | 3–10% | Quality financials, REITs, industrials |
| Other international | 0–10% | Opportunistic |

**Currency risk note:** Positions in non-USD markets create FX exposure. For a long-term value investor, currency effects tend to average out over time. Don't hedge as a matter of routine — it costs returns. Focus on business quality.

### Sector Diversification

Avoid excessive concentration in any one sector. Recommended ceilings:

| Sector | Maximum | Rationale |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| Any single sector | 30% | Sector-specific risk concentration |
| Financial services (banks, insurance) | 25% | Correlated in crises |
| Technology | 35% | High quality but valuation-sensitive |
| Energy / Resources | 15% | Commodity price risk |
| Real Estate | 20% | Rate-sensitive |

**Cyclical vs. defensive balance:**
- Minimum 30% in defensive businesses (consumer staples, healthcare, quality brands)
- This provides stability during recessions and enables rebalancing into cyclicals at distressed prices

## Building the Portfolio

### Step 1: The Candidate Universe

Start with a watchlist of 30–50 high-quality businesses across all target markets. These should all pass the moat screen and financial quality threshold from the analysis frameworks. You buy from this list when price provides a margin of safety.

**Sources for generating candidates:**
- Berkshire Hathaway 13F filings (Buffett's US portfolio)
- Value Investors Club write-ups
- GuruFocus guru portfolios (Munger, Li Lu, Mohnish Pabrai, etc.)
- Screener.in (India quality screens)
- Barron's, Value Investor Insight
- High-ROIC screens: ROE > 20%, consistent for 10 years, across target markets

### Step 2: Quality Filter

Every candidate must pass these minimum bars before portfolio entry:

**Quantitative gates:**
- [ ] 10-year average ROE > 15%
- [ ] 10-year average ROIC > 12%
- [ ] FCF conversion > 70% (FCF/Net Income)
- [ ] Debt/Equity < 1.0 (non-financials) or Tier 1 ratio > 10% (banks)
- [ ] EPS positive in at least 8 of last 10 years (no consistent losses)
- [ ] Revenue CAGR > 5% over 10 years

**Qualitative gates:**
- [ ] Identifiable, durable competitive moat (score ≥ 18/30 on moat scorecard)
- [ ] Management demonstrably allocates capital well
- [ ] Business understandable (within circle of competence)
- [ ] No major integrity concerns (related party abuse, restatements, investigations)

### Step 3: Valuation and Entry

Only buy when the margin of safety is adequate. The price you pay determines your return.

**Entry discipline:**
- Calculate intrinsic value range
- Only buy at a price that provides the required margin of safety (see intrinsic value reference)
- If a great business is fully valued: add it to watchlist, wait for a pullback
- Use market volatility as your friend — crashes and corrections create buying opportunities

**Averaging down:**
- Only average down if: (a) the business fundamentals are intact, and (b) you've re-verified the thesis
- Never average down into a deteriorating business
- If averaging down, the additional purchase should be larger than the initial (higher conviction at lower prices)

## Portfolio Management

### Monitoring Cadence

| Activity | Frequency |
|----------|-----------|
| Review all positions vs. thesis | Quarterly (after earnings) |
| Recalculate intrinsic value | Semi-annually or on major news |
| Watchlist price checks | Monthly |
| Full re-analysis of each holding | Annually |
| Portfolio-level review | Annually |

### When to Add
- Price drops below margin of safety entry point for a high-quality business
- New information increases your conviction (strong earnings, moat confirmation)
- Market crash or sector selloff creates broad opportunity

### When to Trim
- Position exceeds 20% of portfolio (trim to 15%)
- Business has materially re-rated: price is now significantly above intrinsic value
- Allocation shift: better opportunity elsewhere requires capital

### When to Sell (Fisher's discipline)
Sell only when one of three conditions is met:

1. **Thesis is broken**: The fundamental reason you bought has changed (moat eroding, management integrity issue, structural industry decline). This is NOT a price reason.

2. **Significantly overvalued**: Price is > 50% above your most optimistic intrinsic value estimate AND there are better opportunities available.

3. **Better opportunity**: A demonstrably better risk/reward opportunity exists and portfolio is at maximum position count.

**Do NOT sell because:**
- The stock has dropped (unless thesis is broken)
- Short-term earnings disappointed without fundamental change
- The market is going down generally
- You've already made a good return (let winners run)

## Portfolio Metrics and Reporting

Track these metrics for the overall portfolio:

| Metric | Target | How to Calculate |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| Weighted avg. ROIC | > 15% | Sum(position weight × company ROIC) |
| Weighted avg. P/E | < 20x | Sum(position weight × company P/E) |
| Weighted avg. FCF yield | > 5% | Sum(position weight × FCF/Market Cap) |
| Portfolio Intrinsic Value | | Sum(position size × per-share IV) |
| Portfolio upside to IV | > 20% | (IV − Market Value) / Market Value |
| Geographic exposure | | Sum by region |
| Sector exposure | | Sum by sector |
| Number of positions | 10–20 | Count |

### Annual Report Card

Compare the portfolio against:
1. **MSCI World Index** (global benchmark)
2. **S&P 500** (US benchmark)
3. **Hang Seng Index** (HK benchmark)
4. **Relevant regional indices** for non-US positions

Report over: 1-year, 3-year, 5-year rolling periods.

**Important caveat**: Don't over-optimize for short-term performance. A value portfolio may underperform in bull markets (expensive stocks going higher) and outperform in bear markets and full cycles. Judge success over 5+ year rolling periods.
