# Investment Frameworks: Graham, Buffett, Munger, Fisher

This file contains the core investment principles and scoring checklists for each master investor. Apply them in combination — a great investment typically scores well across all four frameworks.

---

## Benjamin Graham — The Foundation

Graham established the intellectual framework for all value investing. His two key works: *Security Analysis* (1934) and *The Intelligent Investor* (1949).

### Core Principles
1. **Mr. Market**: The market is a manic-depressive business partner who offers to buy or sell you shares daily at varying prices. You are never obligated to transact. Use his irrationality to your advantage — buy when he's panicking, avoid buying when he's euphoric.
2. **Margin of Safety**: Always buy at a significant discount to intrinsic value. The margin of safety is the investor's single most important concept — it compensates for errors in analysis and bad luck.
3. **Investment vs. Speculation**: An investment operation is one which, upon thorough analysis, promises safety of principal and an adequate return. Operations not meeting these requirements are speculative.

### Graham's Quantitative Checklist (Defensive Investor)
Rate each criterion: Pass / Marginal / Fail

| # | Criterion | Threshold | Notes |
|---|-----------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Adequate size | Revenue > $100M or market cap > $500M | Avoid tiny companies |
| 2 | Strong financial condition | Current ratio ≥ 2.0 (industrials) | Long-term debt ≤ net current assets |
| 3 | Earnings stability | Positive EPS every year for 10 years | No loss years |
| 4 | Dividend record | Uninterrupted dividends for 20 years | (optional for growth) |
| 5 | Earnings growth | EPS 10-yr CAGR ≥ 3% | Inflation-adjusted |
| 6 | Moderate P/E | P/E ≤ 15x trailing earnings | Current P/E |
| 7 | Moderate P/B | P/B ≤ 1.5x | Or P/E × P/B ≤ 22.5 |
| 8 | Financial strength | Debt/Equity ≤ 0.5 (industrials) | Conservative balance sheet |

**Graham Net-Net Screen** (deep value, rare today):
- Net Current Asset Value (NCAV) = Current Assets − Total Liabilities
- Buy if Price < 2/3 × NCAV per share
- This is Graham's most conservative approach — typically found in distressed markets

### Graham Formula for Intrinsic Value
```
V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g) × (4.4 / Y)

Where:
  EPS = trailing twelve month earnings per share
  8.5 = base P/E for a no-growth company (Graham's assumption)
  g   = expected annual EPS growth rate (next 7-10 years, be conservative)
  4.4 = Graham's assumed "normal" AAA bond yield (adjust to current yield)
  Y   = current AAA corporate bond yield
```

**Usage**: This formula is a quick first approximation. Cross-check with DCF and EPV. Graham himself said the formula is illustrative, not definitive.

## Warren Buffett — Business Quality First

Buffett synthesized Graham's margin-of-safety framework with Philip Fisher's quality-business emphasis. Key principle: **It's far better to buy a wonderful business at a fair price than a fair business at a wonderful price.**

### The "Wonderful Business" Criteria

A business worth owning has most of these characteristics:

**Economics**
- [ ] ROE consistently > 15% (ideally > 20%) without excessive leverage
- [ ] High and stable operating margins (look for 15%+ in most industries)
- [ ] Low capital requirements to maintain and grow earnings (high asset-light model)
- [ ] Owner earnings grow reliably year after year
- [ ] Pricing power: can raise prices without losing customers

**Competitive Position**
- [ ] "Economic moat" — durable competitive advantage
- [ ] Would still be the dominant player in 10–20 years? (the newspaper test: explain the moat in plain language)
- [ ] Difficult for competitors to replicate the business model

**Management**
- [ ] Acts like an owner, not an employee
- [ ] Capital allocation record: good use of retained earnings (reinvests at high returns, or returns cash)
- [ ] Honest and straightforward in communications (read 5 years of annual letters)
- [ ] Compensation is reasonable and aligned with shareholders
- [ ] No serial acquirer of businesses at high prices

**Simplicity**
- [ ] Business is simple enough to understand (within your circle of competence)
- [ ] Revenue predictable and recurring (not lumpy project-based)
- [ ] Minimal government regulation risk (or regulation as a moat)

### Owner Earnings Calculation
Buffett's preferred earnings metric — more accurate than reported net income:

```
Owner Earnings = Net Income
               + Depreciation & Amortization
               + Other Non-Cash Charges
               − Average Annual Capital Expenditures Required to Maintain Business
               ± Changes in Working Capital

Note: "Capex required to maintain" is the key judgment call.
For an asset-light business (software, consumer brands), maintenance capex << D&A.
For capital-intensive businesses (railroads, utilities), maintenance capex ≈ D&A.
```

### Buffett's 10-Year Earnings Test
Project owner earnings 10 years out using conservative growth rates, discount at 10% (or current long-term Treasury + 3%), compare to current market cap.

**Rule of thumb**: If you can buy the business for 10x owner earnings and it can grow those earnings at 10%+ for 10 years, you likely have a good investment.

### Buffett Red Flags (pass immediately)
- Management that uses creative accounting or adjusts for "non-recurring" items every year
- Excessive share issuance to fund acquisitions
- Frequent restructuring charges
- Acquisition-led growth without organic earnings power
- Complex financial structures that obscure the economics
- Compensation schemes that disconnect management from shareholder outcomes

## Charlie Munger — Mental Models & Inversion

Munger's contribution is applying a broad latticework of mental models from psychology, economics, biology, and physics to investment decisions. His most quoted insight: "Invert, always invert."

### Key Mental Models for Investing

**1. Inversion (Most Important)**
Instead of asking "why will this business succeed?", ask:
- *What would have to be true for this business to fail catastrophically?*
- *What are the ways the moat could be eroded?*
- *What could make this management team destroy value?*
- *What macro or regulatory changes would eliminate the business model?*

If the answers are "unlikely or hard to imagine," that's a good sign. If the answers are easy and obvious, be cautious.

**2. Circle of Competence**
Explicitly identify whether you understand this business well enough to predict its economics in 10 years. Be honest.
- Rate understanding: Deep / Moderate / Surface / Outside competence
- If outside competence: either study it until you understand it, or skip it
- "I don't understand it" is a perfectly valid investment decision

**3. Lollapalooza Effect**
When multiple forces reinforce each other, effects are multiplicative, not additive. Look for businesses where:
- High switching costs + pricing power + brand loyalty = much stronger moat than any alone
- Network effects + data advantages + switching costs = near-unassailable position

**4. The Psychology of Human Misjudgment (applicable to investment)**
- **Social proof bias**: Don't buy because everyone else is buying (bubbles)
- **Availability bias**: Don't over-weight recent news (short-term thinking)
- **Anchoring**: Don't anchor on a stock's 52-week high/low as a reference for value
- **Commitment bias**: Be willing to change your thesis if facts change
- **Incentive bias**: Always ask "who benefits from telling me this, and how?"

**5. Opportunity Cost**
Every investment must be compared against alternatives. A good business at a high price might be worse than a mediocre business at a low price when alternatives are considered.

### Munger's Business Quality Checklist
- [ ] Is the business simple and understandable? (if complex, who benefits?)
- [ ] Does management show evidence of rationality in capital allocation?
- [ ] Is the competitive position strengthening or weakening?
- [ ] What are the incentive structures? Are they aligned?
- [ ] What would a smart, well-funded competitor need to do to take market share?
- [ ] Is the business getting better (higher margins, returns) or maintaining?
- [ ] Are there any regulatory, technological, or social trends that could erode the business?

### Munger's "Too Hard" Pile
Munger famously uses a "too hard" pile for investments he can't fully understand or predict. Apply this ruthlessly:
- Turnaround situations with uncertain outcomes → too hard
- Businesses dependent on one government contract or regulation → too hard
- Companies in rapid technological change (competitive dynamics unclear) → too hard
- Highly leveraged financial companies → too hard (unless deep expertise)

## Philip Fisher — Growth Quality & Scuttlebutt

Fisher's *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* (1958) introduced qualitative research methods that Buffett credits as "15% Fisher, 85% Graham" (in his early career, later inverted).

### Fisher's 15 Points Checklist

Score each: Strong / Adequate / Weak / Unknown

**Products & Markets**
1. [ ] Does the company have products/services with sufficient market potential for a sizeable increase in sales over several years?
2. [ ] Does management have determination to continue developing products/services to further increase total sales after current growth products mature?
3. [ ] How effective are the company's R&D efforts relative to its size?

**People & Management**
4. [ ] Does the company have an above-average sales organization?
5. [ ] Does the company have a worthwhile profit margin?
6. [ ] What is the company doing to maintain or improve profit margins?
7. [ ] Does the company have outstanding labor and personnel relations? (high turnover = bad sign)
8. [ ] Does the company have outstanding executive relations? (promote from within, retain talent?)
9. [ ] Does the company have depth to its management? (not dependent on one person)
10. [ ] How good are the company's cost analysis and accounting controls?

**Financial & Competitive**
11. [ ] Are there other aspects of the business, somewhat peculiar to the industry, which will give the investor important clues as to how outstanding the company may be in relation to its competition?
12. [ ] Does the company have a short-range or long-range outlook in regard to profits?
13. [ ] In the foreseeable future will the growth of the company necessitate sufficient equity financing so that the larger number of shares outstanding will largely cancel the existing stockholders' benefit from this anticipated growth?
14. [ ] Does management talk freely to investors about its affairs when things are going well but "clam up" when troubles and disappointments occur?
15. [ ] Does the company have management of unquestionable integrity?

### The Scuttlebutt Method
Fisher's key research technique: go beyond financial statements and talk to or read about everyone connected to the business.

**Sources to investigate:**
- **Customers**: Do they love the product? Would they switch if a competitor offered 10% lower price?
- **Competitors**: What do rivals say about this company's strengths/weaknesses?
- **Employees (ex)**: Glassdoor reviews, LinkedIn patterns, news about culture
- **Suppliers**: Does the company treat suppliers fairly? Is it a desirable customer?
- **Industry analysts/experts**: Seek out independent experts who know the industry
- **Academic/trade press**: Industry journals, trade publications

**In practice (web-based scuttlebutt):**
1. Search `"[company] reviews" site:glassdoor.com` — employee sentiment
2. Search `"[product] review"` — customer satisfaction
3. Search `"[company] supplier"` — supply chain relationships
4. Read competitor earnings calls — what do rivals say about the competitive environment?
5. Read industry trade publications for expert commentary

### Fisher's "When to Sell" (equally important)
Fisher almost never sold — but his criteria for selling:
1. You made a mistake in original analysis (facts have changed, not your price)
2. The company no longer qualifies under the 15 points
3. A clearly superior opportunity exists and the portfolio needs to be concentrated

**Fisher never sold because of price alone.** If the business quality remains excellent, holding through market declines is often the right decision.

## Combined Scoring Framework

Apply all four frameworks and produce a summary score:

| Framework | Score (1–10) | Key Positives | Key Concerns |

| Buffett (business quality) | | | |
| Munger (mental models + inversion) | | | |
| Fisher (qualitative + people) | | | |
| **Overall** | **/10** | | |

**Score interpretation:**
- 8–10: Outstanding — strong buy candidate if price permits
- 6–7: Good business — buy if meaningful margin of safety exists
- 4–5: Mediocre — Graham-style deep value might work; quality play unlikely
- 1–3: Avoid — structural issues, management concerns, or deteriorating economics

## Warning Signs Across All Frameworks

These are immediate disqualifiers or require extraordinary justification to overcome:

| Warning Sign | Framework | Implication |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| Revenue recognition manipulation | All | Integrity issue |
| Aggressive goodwill or intangible capitalization | Graham | Inflated assets |
| ROE driven by excessive leverage | Buffett/Graham | Fragile returns |
| CEO enrichment through complex comp | Buffett/Munger | Agency problem |
| Answers to analyst questions are evasive | Fisher/Munger | Transparency issue |
| Business model requires constant equity raises | All | Value destruction |
| Promoter pledging shares (India) | All | Financial distress |
| Frequent management turnover | Fisher | Culture/leadership issue |
| Auditor changes without explanation | All | Serious red flag |
| VIE structures with unclear economics | Munger | Complexity/risk |
